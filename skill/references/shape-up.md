# Shape Up

Lessons from Basecamp's Shape Up methodology on scoping, shipping, and making bets instead of managing backlogs.

## Fixed time, variable scope

**Core insight:** Set the time, then fit the work into it. Not the other way around. The appetite (how much time you're willing to spend) is the constraint, not the estimate (how long you think it will take). Estimates are guesses. Appetite is a decision.

**Applications:**

- Start every project by asking "how much time is this worth?" not "how long will this take?"
- A six-week appetite means you ship in six weeks or you stop. The scope flexes to fit.
- This kills scope creep by design. When time is fixed, every addition means a subtraction.
- "Nice to haves" get cut automatically because the constraint is real and immovable
- If the work doesn't fit the appetite, reshape it or don't bet on it. Don't extend the timeline.

**When to invoke:** When someone is estimating instead of deciding. When a project keeps growing because the deadline keeps moving.

**Challenge phrase:** "How much time is this worth to you? Now, what fits in that time?"

## Shaping before building

**Core insight:** Don't hand off a feature request. Hand off a shaped problem. Shaping means defining the boundaries, the approach, and the rabbit holes to avoid before anyone writes code. It's the work before the work, and skipping it is how projects balloon.

**Applications:**

- A shaped problem has clear boundaries: what's in, what's out, and what the rough solution looks like
- Shaping is not a spec. It's abstract enough to leave room for the builder, concrete enough to avoid dead ends.
- Unshaped work is a blank check. "Build notifications" could take a week or three months. Shape it first.
- Identify the risks and unknowns upfront. Flag the parts that could blow up the timeline.
- If you can't shape it, you don't understand it well enough to build it

**When to invoke:** When someone jumps straight from idea to implementation. When the scope of a feature is described in one sentence without boundaries.

**Challenge phrase:** "What's the shaped version of this? What's in, what's out, and where are the risks?"

## The circuit breaker

**Core insight:** If it doesn't ship in the cycle, it doesn't ship. No extensions. No "just one more week." The circuit breaker is what prevents projects from dragging on indefinitely. It forces honest scoping upfront because you know there's no safety net.

**Applications:**

- A hard deadline with no extensions changes how you build. You cut scope early instead of late.
- If a project gets to the end of its cycle unfinished, that's information. It was either poorly shaped or not worth the appetite.
- Killing unfinished work feels wasteful but it's cheaper than continuing a project that's already over budget
- The circuit breaker protects your future. Every week spent finishing an overdue project is a week stolen from the next bet.
- Teams that don't use circuit breakers accumulate zombie projects that consume resources indefinitely

**When to invoke:** When someone is extending a deadline for the second time. When "almost done" has been the status for too long.

**Challenge phrase:** "If this had a hard stop today, what would you ship? What would you cut?"

## Bets, not backlogs

**Core insight:** Every cycle is a fresh bet. Nothing is automatically next. Backlogs create the illusion that work has been prioritized when it's actually just been deferred. A long backlog is a guilt list, not a plan.

**Applications:**

- At the start of each cycle, look at what matters now. Not what mattered three months ago when someone added a ticket.
- If an idea is important, it will come back. You don't need a list to remember it.
- Backlogs grow forever and nobody reads them. They become a graveyard of good intentions.
- Betting means committing fully for the cycle. Not splitting attention across twelve things.
- Say no to most things. The power of a bet is that you chose this over everything else.

**When to invoke:** When someone has a long backlog they're trying to prioritize. When they feel overwhelmed by the number of things "waiting to be done."

**Challenge phrase:** "If you deleted your backlog today, what would you still remember to build? That's your real priority list."

## Cool-down periods

**Core insight:** Breathing room between cycles isn't wasted time. It's where fixes happen, exploration happens, and reassessment happens. Without cool-down, you're always executing and never reflecting. That's how you build the wrong thing efficiently.

**Applications:**

- Use cool-down for bug fixes, small improvements, and tech debt that accumulated during the cycle
- Cool-down is when you investigate new ideas without committing to them. Spike, prototype, explore.
- Teams that skip cool-down burn out. Individuals who skip it lose perspective.
- Cool-down is also where you ask: did the last cycle's bet pay off? What did we learn?
- Don't schedule cool-down work. The point is unstructured time. Let people gravitate to what matters.

**When to invoke:** When someone is starting a new feature immediately after shipping the last one. When there's no space between build cycles for reflection.

**Challenge phrase:** "When was the last time you stopped building and asked whether you're building the right thing?"
