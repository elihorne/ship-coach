# Product-Market Fit and Growth

Insights on finding fit, knowing when to launch, and making hard product decisions.

## The only thing that matters: product-market fit

**Core insight:** Product-market fit means being in a good market with a product that can satisfy that market. When you have it, you feel it. When you don't, you feel that too.

**Signals you have PMF:**

- Users are telling other users about you (organic growth)
- Usage metrics are growing without much marketing
- Customers complain when the product is down
- You can't hire fast enough to keep up with demand

**Signals you don't have PMF:**

- Every customer requires convincing
- Usage is flat or declining
- Customers churn quickly
- You're explaining why people should want this

**When to invoke:** When someone is optimizing before validating. When they're building features without evidence of fit.

**Challenge phrase:** "Do you have PMF yet? If not, what will tell you when you do?"

## Good product management vs. bad product management

**Good PM behaviors for shipping:**

- Knows the market, the product, and the competition cold
- Focuses on the minimal product that solves the problem
- Ships things. Is not measured by volume of features.
- Takes responsibility for outcomes, not activities
- Defines success crisply and measures it

**Bad PM behaviors (red flags):**

- Adds features because competitors have them
- Measures success by shipping, not by impact
- Blames engineering for delays
- Builds what stakeholders ask for without questioning
- Can't say what "done" looks like

**When to invoke:** When someone is taking orders instead of driving outcomes. When the roadmap is a feature list rather than a hypothesis list.

**Challenge phrase:** "What outcome does this feature drive? How will you measure it?"

## When to launch

**Core insight:** Launch early, but not so early you can't learn. The bar is: can users do the core thing?

**The right time to launch:**

- Core functionality works (even if rough)
- You can support a small number of users
- You'll learn something from their behavior
- It's not going to actively harm your brand

**Too early:**

- Core functionality is broken
- You can't actually deliver the value proposition
- Users can't complete the main task

**Too late:**

- You're polishing edges instead of testing assumptions
- You're waiting for "one more feature"
- You're afraid of criticism

**When to invoke:** When someone is unsure if they're ready to launch.

**Challenge phrase:** "Can a user accomplish the main job? Yes? Then launch."

## Speed wins

**Core insight:** Every company is becoming a software company. The opportunity is enormous. Move fast.

**Applications for SaaS:**

- Speed matters. The market is forming now.
- First-mover advantage is real in software markets
- Network effects and switching costs compound
- Being second with a better product often loses to being first with a good-enough product

**When to invoke:** When someone is perfecting instead of claiming territory.

**Challenge phrase:** "Who else is building this? How fast are they moving?"

## On pivots

**Core insight:** Pivoting is a tool, not a failure. But it requires real signal, not frustration.

**When to pivot:**

- Growth has stalled despite effort
- User feedback consistently points elsewhere
- You're excited about a different opportunity you discovered
- The original thesis has been disproven

**When not to pivot:**

- You're frustrated but haven't really tried
- Growth is slow but present
- You're chasing a shinier object
- You haven't talked to enough users

**How to pivot well:**

- Preserve what's working
- Move fast. Pivots should be quick.
- Communicate clearly with users and team
- Don't half-pivot. Commit.

**When to invoke:** When someone is stuck and considering changing direction.

**Challenge phrase:** "Is this a real signal or temporary frustration? What data do you have?"

## Doing hard things

**Core insight:** Startups are hard. The right answer often sucks. Do the hard thing anyway.

**Applications:**

- Cutting scope feels bad but ships products
- Killing features feels wasteful but focuses teams
- Saying no to users feels rude but maintains focus
- Launching scared feels dangerous but generates learning

**When to invoke:** When someone knows what to do but doesn't want to do it.

**Challenge phrase:** "You already know the answer. What's stopping you?"

## On hiring and team size

Relevant to shipping: small teams ship faster.

**Principles:**

- Add people only when you're drowning, not when you're ambitious
- Every person added slows communication
- Two people can ship an MVP. Ten people will debate one.
- Hire after PMF, not before

**When to invoke:** When someone is thinking about hiring to go faster.

**Challenge phrase:** "Will more people actually speed this up? Or slow it down with coordination costs?"
