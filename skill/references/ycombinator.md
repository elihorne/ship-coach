# YC Wisdom

Core lessons from Y Combinator partners and alumni on building and shipping products.

## Default alive vs. default dead

**Core insight:** There are two kinds of startups. Default alive startups will become profitable before running out of money at current growth and spending. Default dead startups won't. Most founders don't know which one they are, and that's dangerous.

**Applications:**

- Calculate your runway honestly. Include the bad scenarios.
- If you're default dead, every feature that doesn't move you toward revenue is a luxury you can't afford
- Default dead means cut scope, not add features
- Survival mode means shipping what pays, not what's interesting
- The question isn't "can we build this" but "can we afford to build this before it pays for itself"

**When to invoke:** When someone is building features without revenue pressure. When they're ignoring burn rate or pretending runway is infinite.

**Challenge phrase:** "Are you default alive or default dead? How does this feature change the answer?"

## The 10x better rule

**Core insight:** Your product needs to be dramatically better than the alternative, not marginally better. Users have switching costs, habits, and inertia. A product that's 20% better isn't worth the effort of changing. A product that's 10x better makes switching obvious.

**Applications:**

- Don't compete on features. Compete on magnitude of improvement.
- Pick one dimension and be wildly better at it. Speed, simplicity, price, or access.
- If you can't articulate what's 10x better about your product, you don't have a product yet
- "Better" is defined by the user, not by you. Ask them.
- Incremental improvements attract incremental interest. Dramatic improvements create word of mouth.

**When to invoke:** When someone is building a slightly better version of something that already exists. When the value proposition is "like X but with Y."

**Challenge phrase:** "What's 10x better about this? If you can't answer immediately, the user can't either."

## Talk to users

**Core insight:** You should be talking to users more than you think. Before you build, while you build, and after you ship. Most founders talk to users too little and build too much. The ratio should be uncomfortable.

**Applications:**

- Talk to users before writing any code. Understand the problem in their words, not yours.
- Five user conversations will teach you more than five weeks of building
- Watch users use your product. Don't ask what they want. Watch what they do.
- Users will tell you problems honestly. They will not design solutions honestly. Listen to the problem, ignore the proposed solution.
- If you haven't talked to a user this week, you're building blind

**When to invoke:** When someone is deep in a build cycle without recent user contact. When feature decisions are based on assumptions rather than conversations.

**Challenge phrase:** "When did you last talk to a user? What did they tell you that surprised you?"

## Do things that don't scale

**Core insight:** Startups win by doing things that big companies can't or won't do. Manual, unscalable work is an advantage when you're small. It gives you learning that automation can't.

**Applications:**

- Recruit users one by one. Email them personally. Onboard them yourself.
- Delight early users excessively. Do things that seem unreasonably attentive.
- Make things manually before automating. Concierge the service before building the feature.
- The goal is to get feedback, not efficiency. Efficiency comes after you know what to build.
- Manual processes reveal what to automate. Automating first means guessing.

**When to invoke:** When someone is overengineering instead of launching. When they're building systems before they have users.

**Challenge phrase:** "How would you deliver this value manually for your first 10 users?"

## The MVP bar is lower than you think

**Core insight:** Your minimum viable product should make you uncomfortable. If you're not embarrassed by it, you waited too long. The point of an MVP is to start learning, not to impress anyone.

**Applications:**

- The MVP is the smallest thing that lets you test your core hypothesis. Nothing more.
- A working product with one feature beats a polished product that doesn't exist
- Ship the version that makes you cringe. Users care about the problem being solved, not the polish.
- Every day you don't launch is a day you're not learning from real usage
- The first version of most successful products was terrible. That's fine. Terrible and live beats beautiful and unreleased.

**When to invoke:** When someone keeps pushing back the launch date. When "almost ready" has been the status for more than a week.

**Challenge phrase:** "What would break if you launched today? Is that actually fatal, or just embarrassing?"

## Retention is the foundation

**Core insight:** Acquisition without retention is a leaky bucket. It doesn't matter how many users you bring in if they leave. Fix retention before you invest in growth. A product people come back to will grow. A product people abandon won't, no matter how much you spend on acquisition.

**Applications:**

- Measure retention before measuring growth. Weekly retention tells you if the product works.
- If users try it once and don't come back, adding features won't help. Fix the core experience.
- Retention is the clearest signal of product-market fit. If people keep using it, you're onto something.
- Don't pour users into a leaky bucket. Plug the holes first.
- Ask churned users why they left. The answer is usually simpler and more painful than you expect.

**When to invoke:** When someone is focused on acquisition, marketing, or virality before proving retention. When growth tactics are discussed before the product retains users.

**Challenge phrase:** "What's your weekly retention rate? If you don't know, that's the first thing to fix."
