# Paul Graham Wisdom

Distilled insights from PG essays relevant to shipping SaaS products.

## Do Things That Don't Scale

The most important essay for early-stage founders.

**Core insight:** Startups win by doing things that big companies can't or won't do. Manual, unscalable work is an advantage when you're small.

**Applications:**

- Recruit users one by one. Email them personally. Onboard them yourself.
- Delight early users excessively. Do things that seem unreasonably attentive.
- Make things manually before automating. Concierge the service before building the feature.
- The goal is to get feedback, not efficiency. Efficiency comes after you know what to build.

**When to invoke:** When someone is overengineering instead of launching. When they're building systems before they have users.

**Challenge phrase:** "How would you deliver this value manually for your first 10 users?"

## Launch Fast

From "The 18 Mistakes That Kill Startups" and other essays.

**Core insight:** If you're not embarrassed by your first version, you've launched too late.

**Applications:**

- A working product with one feature beats a polished product that doesn't exist
- You don't know what users want. Launch and find out.
- Every day you don't launch is a day you're not learning
- The market doesn't care about your feelings. Ship and let them tell you what's wrong.

**When to invoke:** When someone is polishing instead of shipping. When they're waiting for "just one more thing."

**Challenge phrase:** "What would break if you launched today? Is that actually fatal?"

## Frighteningly Ambitious Startup Ideas

**Core insight:** The best ideas seem bad at first. If it seems obviously good, someone else would be doing it.

**Applications:**

- Don't worry if your MVP seems too small or unimpressive
- Start with a small wedge that you can dominate
- The "toy" dismissal is often a sign you're onto something
- Make something a few people love rather than something many people like

**When to invoke:** When someone is worried their MVP isn't impressive enough. When they're trying to boil the ocean.

**Challenge phrase:** "Who are the 100 people who will love this? Start there."

## Schlep Blindness

**Core insight:** Founders avoid hard, boring problems even when those problems are valuable. The schlep is often the moat.

**Applications:**

- Tedious problems have less competition
- If it's annoying to build, it's annoying for competitors too
- Don't chase the "clever" solution. Chase the valuable one.
- Integration work, data entry, manual processes, these are opportunities

**When to invoke:** When someone is avoiding the boring but necessary work. When they're chasing exciting features over core functionality.

**Challenge phrase:** "What's the boring problem you're avoiding? Is that the real product?"

## Default Alive or Default Dead

**Core insight:** Are you going to run out of money before becoming profitable? If yes, you're default dead and should act accordingly.

**Applications:**

- Know your runway. Be honest about it.
- Default dead means cut scope, not add features
- Every feature that doesn't contribute to revenue is a luxury
- Survival mode means shipping what pays, not what's interesting

**When to invoke:** When someone is building features without revenue pressure. When they're ignoring unit economics.

**Challenge phrase:** "How does this feature move you toward profitability?"

## How to Get Startup Ideas

**Core insight:** The best ideas come from problems you personally have. Live in the future and build what's missing.

**Applications:**

- Build for yourself first. You understand the problem best.
- Organic ideas (from real problems) beat "made up" ideas
- Notice what annoys you. That's where products live.
- Talk to users, but remember, they don't know what they want either

**When to invoke:** When someone is guessing at features. When they're building what they think users want without evidence.

**Challenge phrase:** "Is this a problem you have? Have you talked to someone who does?"

## Startups = Growth

**Core insight:** A startup is a company designed to grow fast. If you're not growing, you're a small business (which is fine, but different).

**Applications:**

- Pick a metric that matters. Focus on growing it weekly.
- 5-7% weekly growth is good. Measure it.
- Growth reveals whether you have product-market fit
- If growth stalls, the product isn't working. Don't add features, ask why.

**When to invoke:** When someone is building without measuring. When they're adding features without growth data.

**Challenge phrase:** "What's your weekly growth rate? What moves it?"

## Ramen Profitability

**Core insight:** Reach the point where the company can pay your living expenses as fast as possible. This changes everything.

**Applications:**

- Profitability is freedom. You control your destiny.
- Don't raise money to avoid hard choices. Make the hard choices.
- A small but profitable product beats a large unprofitable one
- Charge from day one. Price is a signal.

**When to invoke:** When someone is burning runway on nice-to-haves. When they're not charging yet.

**Challenge phrase:** "When does this become ramen profitable? What's the shortest path?"
