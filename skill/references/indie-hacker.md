# Indie Hacker Wisdom

Lessons from solo builders and bootstrapped founders on shipping fast, staying lean, and building profitably.

## Ship in hours, not weeks

**Core insight:** The best way to validate an idea is to ship it. Not to plan it, research it, or build a roadmap for it. Ship something today. If it takes more than a weekend, you're overbuilding for the amount of certainty you have.

**Applications:**

- Treat every idea as a hypothesis with a short deadline. Build the smallest version that tests it.
- The 12 startups in 12 months model works because most ideas fail. The faster you test, the faster you find what works.
- Public building creates accountability. Announce what you're shipping and when. The deadline forces cuts.
- If you can't ship a testable version in a few days, your scope is too big or your tools are too slow
- Iteration speed is your primary competitive advantage as a solo builder. Protect it ruthlessly.

**When to invoke:** When someone is spending weeks on a first version. When planning has replaced building.

**Challenge phrase:** "Could you ship a version of this today? What would it look like?"

## Charge early and often

**Core insight:** Free users give feedback. Paying users give signal. There is a massive difference between someone saying "this is cool" and someone handing you money. Revenue is the only validation that matters.

**Applications:**

- Charge from day one, even if it's $5/month. The act of paying changes the relationship.
- "I'd pay for this" is worthless. "Here's my credit card" is data.
- Pricing is a feature. It tells you who your real users are and what they actually value.
- Free tiers attract users who will never pay. Be careful about optimizing for them.
- If nobody will pay, that's a signal. Better to learn it now than after six months of free growth.

**When to invoke:** When someone is building a free product and planning to monetize "later." When pricing is being avoided or deferred.

**Challenge phrase:** "Has anyone paid you yet? If not, what's stopping you from charging today?"

## Stay small by default

**Core insight:** One person can run a business doing $1M or more in annual revenue. Headcount is a vanity metric. Every hire adds communication overhead, slower decisions, and higher burn. Stay small unless growth genuinely demands it.

**Applications:**

- The goal is profit per person, not headcount. A solo founder making $500k/year is outperforming a team of 10 making the same.
- Hiring to go faster often makes you slower. Coordination costs are real and they compound.
- Automate before you hire. Use tools, scripts, and services to handle what you'd otherwise need people for.
- If you hire, hire for things you genuinely cannot do yourself and that block growth. Not for comfort.
- "We need to hire" is often code for "we need to focus." Try focus first.

**When to invoke:** When someone is planning hires before achieving profitability. When team size is growing faster than revenue.

**Challenge phrase:** "What would break if you stayed solo for another six months? Is that actually a problem?"

## Build in public

**Core insight:** Building in public is accountability, distribution, and user research rolled into one practice. When you share what you're building, you get feedback before you ship, attention when you do, and a record of decisions that helps you learn.

**Applications:**

- Share progress, not polish. People connect with the process, not the product photos.
- Public building creates a forcing function for shipping. You said you'd launch Friday, so you launch Friday.
- Your build log is marketing. Every update is a touchpoint with potential users.
- Transparency builds trust. Users root for builders they can see working.
- The fear of public failure is exactly the pressure that makes you ship. Use it.

**When to invoke:** When someone is building in isolation. When they're waiting until something is "ready" to show anyone.

**Challenge phrase:** "Who knows you're building this? What would change if you shared your progress today?"

## The solo builder advantage

**Core insight:** Solo builders have no meetings, no consensus requirements, and no coordination overhead. You can ship when the code is ready, not when the sprint ends. This is a genuine competitive advantage, not a limitation to overcome.

**Applications:**

- Decisions happen instantly. You see a problem, you fix it, you ship it. No standups required.
- You own the entire stack, which means you understand the entire system. This makes debugging faster and architecture simpler.
- No politics, no bikeshedding, no "let's circle back." Just building.
- The tradeoff is loneliness and blind spots. Compensate with user conversations and public building, not by hiring.
- Your speed advantage disappears the moment you add process. Add process only when the pain of not having it is real and measurable.

**When to invoke:** When a solo builder is mimicking team processes they don't need. When they're adding structure before complexity demands it.

**Challenge phrase:** "Are you adding this process because you need it, or because you think you should have it?"
